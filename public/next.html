Put a real model behind the tools

Going live changes almost nothing, and that's the point. The model's only new job is choosing which atomic tool to call; the tools, the gate, the audit trail, and the eval set stay exactly where they are. In agent terms the demo is a deterministic Category-1 agent; a real LLM makes it a Category-2 ReAct agent that decides sequence, but the tool surface and write policy are the same contract.

The open engineering question there isn't capability, it's economics: tool-selection is a bounded, cheap call, so you tier the model to the task, a premium model for the ambiguous judgment turns, a cheaper model for routine routing, routed through one gateway (pass-through pricing) so the swap is a config change. A copilot turn runs ~1.5K in / ~300 out, under a cent on a premium model like Sonnet 5 and roughly a sixth of that on an open model like GLM 5.2. At those numbers inference is a rounding error against the eval gate and the governed write path. The model is a commodity, and deflating; the record and the governance are the moat.

The v2 sequence

Same gate, new tools

Each new copilot is additive by construction: new atomic tools and new eval cases behind the unchanged write path. The seating copilot and guest copilot come next — deferred from v1 not because they’re hard but because to-do/timeline is the weekly loop and seating is episodic, a late-stage burst that wouldn’t move weekly return. When they land, the gate, the audit trail, and the approval contract don’t change; the eval file grows.

The governance work that does become load-bearing at v2 is per-actor auth: once the couple, the planner, and a vendor can all write, “who may trigger which tool, and whose approval gates it” is the schema’s next migration — write policy by value × reversibility, now per actor. That’s the same governed-record thesis, one ring further out.

Roadmap and offering preview

The next surfaces are planner-capacity products.

  • v2 seating copilot Read guest constraints, table preferences, and vendor dependencies; propose narrow table changes; stop for approval before changing the chart.
  • Guest and RSVP copilot Turn late RSVP movement into headcount, budget, seating, and vendor-impact drafts instead of leaving that reconciliation in the planner’s inbox.
  • Planner console Rank attention across the client record by vendor risk and client-visible dependencies, so one planner can carry more active weddings without losing taste.
  • White-label planner packaging Let planners sell the experience under their own brand while Hitch supplies the governed record, audit trail, and approval path underneath.

The business

B2B2C: the couple is the wedge, the planner is the buyer

The planner pays us; the couple uses us. Every couple action lands in the record the planner runs their business on, so the couple’s weekly re-engagement compounds into the planner’s capacity — one planner runs more weddings without diluting their taste. That’s the sell on each side of the seam: to couples, the assistant that actually knows your wedding; to planners, capacity without headcount.

The expand is services on top of a record that acts: white-label packaging under the planner’s own brand is v2 — their client experience, our governed record and write path underneath. Deferred deliberately; the v1 job is proving the couple returns weekly and the planner trusts the writes. The PRD carries the metric, the counter-metric, and the kill test that keep that honest.
