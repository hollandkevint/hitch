Part 1 of the planner OS · system of record

A sample grilled session

ChatGPT would take the couple’s wish list at face value and hand back a generic checklist. A senior planner does the opposite: she interviews, she pushes back, and she names the trade-off before it becomes a $9,000 surprise. Step through the same session the Hitch agent runs — one move at a time, each with a recommendation, every answer written to the record.

Frame · get the real constraints

HitchBefore I build anything, I need the constraints, not the mood board. You told me: $40,000 all-in, about 160 guests, black-tie, a Saturday evening in October, on the water in Charleston. Two of those five are going to fight. Let me show you exactly where, because it’s cheaper to know now than in March.

Pressure · name the contradiction

HitchLowcountry Catering’s plated black-tie minimum runs about $185 a head. 160 guests is $29,600 — 74% of your budget on dinner alone, before the venue, florals, photography, music, or your dress. The planners I’ve watched keep catering near 40%. This isn’t a florist problem or a band problem. It’s a guest-count problem.RecommendedHold black-tie and the harbor — that’s the night you actually want — and cut the list to 120.

Sarah & Marcus120 feels small… but honestly we couldn’t name the last 40 anyway.

guest_count: 160 → 120 · decision: guest-list → locked

Hitch120 pulls catering to about $22,000 — 49%, livable. Now florals. You asked for hanging installations over every table. From Petal & Stem that’s $6,500–$8,000, roughly 18%; the norm is 8–10%.RecommendedOne statement installation over the head table where the photos happen, low greenery everywhere else. The ontology · what the grill writes into

The domain model a planner already carries

The grill isn’t chat. It writes into a model a seasoned planner keeps in her head — entities, sequencing lanes, and budget norms. This is the context a general model doesn’t have and can’t retain between conversations.

Sequencing lanes

  1. Venue + dateLocked first — everything downstream is relative to them.
  2. Catering headcountSet by guest_count; drives the single biggest line.
  3. RentalsFollow headcount — tables, linens, place settings scale with it.
  4. FloralsAfter the venue walk-through, once the room is known.
  5. AttireOrdered against an 8-week alteration floor, or it’s a crisis.
  6. Save-the-dates6 months out — earlier is noise, later loses the block.
  7. Final headcount2 weeks out; locks catering and seating counts.
  8. SeatingLast — it depends on everything above it.

Why the order is the product

“What’s next” is real order, not a generic list. A change upstream (guest count) cascades down (catering, rentals, seating) on its own — the planner doesn’t re-derive it and the couple can’t book the florist before the room is known.

Norms are defaults, not laws

The music line breaks the norm on purpose because the couple ranked dancing first. The model records the override and the reason, so a later reviewer sees a decision, not a mistake.

Closeout is a state walk

Part 3 walks every vendor to paid → closed and every decision to invoiced. That is what turns a plan into a settled, billable record — and what a chat transcript can never become.
